Why quantum computing?
1:05  ·  fin à 3:15

Why quantum computing?

Classical machine learning is excellent.

But it has three limits here.

It tests hypotheses one by one.

It cannot see distant correlations alone.

We must build the right features by hand.

And it is fragile under attack.

Quantum computing offers three matching properties.

Superposition: many configurations explored at the same time.

Entanglement: distant correlations, captured in a native way.

That is exactly our coordinated attack.

And the third property is the bet of this work.

We treat quantum noise as a resource.

Not as an enemy.

These machines are noisy anyway.

Can this noise work for us?

That is the question today.

5
The foundation
1:30  ·  fin à 5:45

Now the foundation, from our earlier paper.

Each neuron is a qubit.

A qubit has two levels.

And it can live in both at the same time.

That is superposition.

The network evolves in a random way.

It follows a stochastic master equation.

montrer l'équation

Three parts.

Part one: the Hamiltonian.

The smooth, regular motion.

Think of a spinning top.

Part two: the Lindblad term.

The friction.

The contact with the environment.

This is decoherence.

Part three: the Wiener term.

Pure randomness.

Small random kicks, all the time.

Like Brownian motion.

The neurons are linked by an Ising interaction.

Pairs of qubits feel each other, like small magnets.

And on top, we inject controlled noise.

Why?

Biological neurons are noisy, and they are robust.

Quantum machines are noisy too.

Maybe noise can become a feature.

We will test this idea with data.

The architecture, four layers
1:05  ·  fin à 6:50

The architecture has four layers.

Layer one: encoding.

Each feature becomes a rotation angle, on its own qubit.

The data is written into the qubits.

Layer two: the entanglement layer.

Entangling gates, arranged in a ring.

This layer is the star of the talk.

Layer three: the core.

Trainable rotations.

These angles are the weights we learn.

Plus the random evolution.

Layer four: the decision.

We measure all the qubits.

We sum the results.

And we get a score.

Zero means normal.

One means anomaly.

A standard circuit, plus two ingredients: the ring, and the noise.

How did I simulate the qubits?

12
Why noise disrupts
1:15  ·  fin à 14:40

Why does it fail?

The dropout analogy breaks at one point.

Classical dropout switches off single neurons, during training.

So each neuron must be useful alone.

Neurons cannot rely on each other.

That is why it works.

Our noise is different.

It disturbs the final measurement.

One number, at the end of the circuit.

It never touches the gates.

In practice, this is like noise on the labels.

Not on the weights.

Label noise builds nothing.

It only corrupts the gradient.

More parameters, more damage.

Exactly the trend we saw.

The good news: the diagnosis gives the cure.

A true quantum dropout should switch off single gates.

A random mask on each rotation.

This is our next step.

Not a dead end.

A new direction.
